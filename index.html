About Ecosystems

  • An ecosystem consists of a community of organisms together with their physical environment.
  • Ecosystems can be of different sizes and can be marine, aquatic, or terrestrial. Broad categories of terrestrial ecosystems are called biomes.
  • In ecosystems, both matter and energy are conserved. Energy flows through the system—usually from light to heat—while matter is recycled.
  • Ecosystems with higher biodiversity tend to be more stable with greater resistance and resilience in the face of disturbances, or disruptive events.

Why Ecosystems Matter

Ecosystems matter because they provide many benefits to humans and the environment, including:

  • Food and water: Ecosystems provide food and water for humans. For example, agriculture depends on the characteristics of an ecosystem, such as temperature and humidity.
  • Climate regulation: Ecosystems regulate the climate.
  • Stress and anxiety reduction: Ecosystems can reduce stress and anxiety.
  • Habitat: Ecosystems provide a habitat for wild plants and animals.
  • Food chains and food webs: Ecosystems support food chains and food webs.
  • Nutrient recycling: Ecosystems recycle nutrients between biotic and abiotic components.
  • Energy flow: Ecosystems maintain the flow of energy through the carbon, water, nitrogen, and oxygen cycles.
  • Evolution: The interactions between species in an ecosystem drive evolution.
